1. ORGANIZATIONAL CULTURE

Definition of Culture

Culture is the unique dominant pattern of shared beliefs, assumptions, values, and norms that shape the socialization, symbols, language and practices of a group of people. It represents the attitudes and approaches that typify the way staff carry out their tasks.

Requirements for Culture to Exist

  1. Must be shared by the vast majority of members of a group or society
  2. Must be passed on from generation to generation
  3. Must shape behaviour and perceptions

Cultural Iceberg Model

Observable Elements Not Observable Elements
Practices, Language, Symbols Norms, Values, Assumptions

Key Components of Culture

Shared Assumptions

Thoughts and feelings that members of a culture take for granted and believe to be true.

Values and Norms

  • Values: Basic beliefs that specify general preferences and behaviours, define what is right and wrong
  • Cultural Values: Reflected in society's morals, customs and established practices
  • Norms: Rules that govern behaviours of groups of people

Symbols

Any visible object, act, or event that conveys meaning to others

  • Artefacts
  • Dress
  • Office layout
  • Slogans
  • Ceremonies
Example: The Nike swoosh symbolizes the Greek goddess Nike (winged goddess of victory) and represents her flight, conveying the brand meaning.

Language

A shared system of vocal sounds, written signs, and/or gestures used to convey meaning among members of a culture.

Practices

Observable cultural customs such as taboos (culturally forbidden behaviours) and ceremonies.

Socialization

The process by which people learn values, norms, behaviours and social skills. It is the means by which new members are brought into a culture.

2. TYPES OF ORGANIZATIONAL CULTURE

Culture Type Characteristics Examples
Bureaucratic Behaviour governed by formal rules and standard operating procedures. Perpetuates stability. Produces standardized goods and services. Government ministries, Fast food establishments
Clan Behaviour shaped by tradition, loyalty, personal commitment, extensive socialization. Unity through socialization. Long-term employees serve as mentors. Strong peer pressure to adhere to norms. Members share pride in membership. Family businesses, organizations with strong traditions
Market Values reflect achieving measurable and demanding goals (financial and market based). Focus on sales growth, profitability, market share. Contractual relationship between individual and organization. Performance-based rewards. Managers judged on performance goals, not mentorship. Sales organizations, competitive businesses
Entrepreneurial High levels of risk taking and creativity. Commitment to experimentation, innovation, being on leading edge. Apple (Steve Jobs), Tech startups

3. CULTURE AND ORGANIZATIONAL PERFORMANCE

Relationship

Organizational culture has the potential to enhance organizational performance, individual satisfaction, and the sense of certainty about how problems are to be handled. Culture serves as a control mechanism to channel behaviour towards desired behaviours and prevent undesired behaviours.

Building a Strong Organizational Culture

  1. A common behavioural style must be shared by managers and employees
  2. Have the same basic approaches to solving problems, meeting goals, and dealing with stakeholders
  3. Share common norms that guide rules governing rewards and punishment
  4. A strong organizational culture assists in creating a stable organization, leading to achievement of strategic goals

Steps to Building Strong Culture (Socialization Process)

Step Action
Step 1 Careful selection
Step 2 Challenging early work assignments
Step 3 Training to develop capabilities with culture
Step 4 Reward and sustain culture
Step 5 Adoption of cultural value policies
Step 6 Reinforce culture with ritual, stories, rites
Step 7 Role model to sustain culture

Outcomes of Socialization Process

Successful Socialization

  • Job satisfaction
  • Role clarity
  • High work performance
  • Understanding of culture
  • Commitment to organization
  • Internal values

Unsuccessful Socialization

  • Job dissatisfaction
  • Role ambiguity and conflict
  • Misunderstanding, tension, perceived lack of control
  • Low job involvement
  • Low performance
  • Rejection of values

4. DIVERSITY IN ORGANIZATIONS

What is Diversity?

Diversity means difference. It refers to the variety of observable and unobservable similarities and differences among people (race, gender, age, etc.). In employment context, it means ensuring organizations recruit and retain the best person from the widest possible talent base regardless of gender, sexual orientation, age, race, religion or disability.

Types of Diversity

Surface Level Diversity Deep Level Diversity
Observable differences that are known as soon as you see someone Individual differences that cannot be seen directly
  • Age
  • Gender
  • Race
  • Ethnicity
  • Socio-Economic background
  • Physical features
  • Physical abilities
  • Religion
  • Marital Status
  • Goals
  • Values
  • Personalities
  • Work preference
  • Decision making styles
  • Attitude
  • Knowledge & Skills
Reflects characteristics observable immediately Takes more time to learn but has strong influence on organizational performance

Benefits of Workforce Diversity

  1. Increased Productivity: Brings diverse talents working towards common goal using different skill sets
  2. Increased Creativity and Problem Solving: Every individual brings their way of thinking, operating and solving problems
  3. Attract and Retain Talent: Adds competitive edge to any organization
  4. Build Synergy in Teams: Brings new attitudes and processes that profit the whole team, enhances communication
  5. Increases Market Share: Creates satisfied diverse customer base

Negative Impact of Diversity

  • Conflicts: People feel confused, threatened or annoyed by individuals with very different views and backgrounds
  • Bureaucracy: Decision-making delayed due to diverging views
  • Unproductivity: Lack of common ground negatively affects team spirit essential for high productivity
  • Disunity: Different opinions on how business should be run and managed

Managing a Diverse Workforce

1. Generation Gap

  • Generation X: Before 1976
  • Generation Y (Echo Boomers/Millennials): 1977-1994
  • Generation Z: 1995-2012

Creates communication gap due to lingo, slang and technology. Requires training for Generation X and Y.

Example: Companies like Infosys, Wipro, Microsoft give regular training to employees.

2. Gender

  • Requirements of men and women are different at workplace
  • Women require more safety and privacy
  • Organizations give minimum 12 weeks maternity leave
Example: Google gives 18 weeks paid leave and up to 5 years unpaid leave.

3. Differently Abled

  • Require special infrastructure for convenience
  • Tasks allocated according to ability
Example: Software companies and service sector provide good environment for differently abled people.

4. Sexual Orientation and Gender Identity

Organizations should take care of LGBT (lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender) employees.

Example: IBM believes strong transgender policies are natural extension of corporate culture.

5. Intellectual Abilities

Employees have different thinking, reasoning and problem-solving approaches. Organizations should give freedom to think in their own ways.

Example: Intel Inc. gives freedom to employees to think differently.

6. Values, Attitude, Personality

To manage deep level diversity, organizations should give individual training.

5. CRITICAL THINKING

What is Critical Thinking?

Critical thinking is the ability to think clearly and logically about a situation. It involves evaluating information, questioning assumptions, and making decisions based on evidence rather than emotions or guesses.

Characteristics of Critical Thinkers

  • Curious
  • Open-minded
  • Willing to challenge ideas
  • Ask strong questions
  • Look for evidence
  • Avoid jumping to conclusions
  • Evaluate arguments logically before accepting them

Steps of Critical Thinking

Step Description
1 Identify the problem
2 Gather relevant information
3 Analyze facts and data
4 Evaluate different options
5 Draw a reasoned conclusion
6 Reflect on the results and learn from them

Importance of Critical Thinking

  • Helps solve complex problems
  • Makes fair decisions
  • Reduces personal bias
  • Understands situations from multiple perspectives
  • Key skill in academics, careers, and daily life

Examples of Critical Thinking

  • Evaluate reliability of a source
  • Compare evidence before deciding
  • Identify flaws in an argument
  • Weigh pros and cons before choosing best solution

How to Improve Critical Thinking

  • Ask deeper questions
  • Analyze evidence
  • Recognize biases
  • Evaluate arguments
  • Reflect on decisions
  • Avoid emotional reactions when judging information

Barriers to Critical Thinking

  • Personal bias and emotions
  • Lack of reliable information
  • Time pressure and stress
  • Fear of questioning authority or common beliefs
  • Overconfidence and assumptions

6. CREATIVE THINKING

What is Creative Thinking?

Creative thinking is the ability to generate new and original ideas. It focuses on imagination, flexibility, and exploring possibilities that go beyond traditional solutions or patterns.

Characteristics of Creative Thinkers

  • Imaginative
  • Curious
  • Open to trying new ideas
  • See opportunities instead of limitations
  • Think in new directions
  • Take risks
  • Challenge ordinary ways of doing things

Types of Creative Thinking

  • Divergent Thinking: Generating multiple ideas
  • Brainstorming: Group idea generation
  • Lateral Thinking: Approaching problems from different angles
  • Metaphorical Thinking: Using analogies and comparisons
  • Inspirational Thinking: Drawing from inspiration

Importance of Creative Thinking

  • Drives innovation in science, art, business, everyday problem-solving
  • Helps discover new solutions
  • Adapt to change
  • Express ideas in original ways

Examples of Creative Thinking

  • Design a new product
  • Write a unique story
  • Develop fresh marketing idea
  • Use unexpected method to solve a problem

How to Improve Creative Thinking

  • Brainstorming
  • Mind mapping
  • Trying new experiences
  • Practicing free writing
  • Challenging assumptions
  • Allowing yourself to think without limits

Barriers to Creative Thinking

  • Fear of failure or criticism
  • Rigid thinking and fixed habits
  • Lack of motivation or inspiration
  • Limited resources or time
  • Overthinking and self-doubt

7. CRITICAL VS CREATIVE THINKING

Aspect Critical Thinking Creative Thinking
Focus Logic, evaluation, judging accuracy Imagination, originality, generating new ideas
Purpose Analyze and judge information logically Generate new and imaginative ideas
Approach Systematic, structured, evidence-based Flexible, exploratory, possibility-focused
When to Use When you need to analyze, evaluate, or judge information When you need new ideas or alternative solutions

How They Work Together

Both are different but equally important. Together they create powerful problem-solving abilities. Most successful projects combine both at different stages. Creative thinking generates ideas, while critical thinking filters and improves them. This combination helps us come up with innovative solutions that are also practical, realistic, and effective.

Real-Life Applications

  • Business strategy
  • Academic work
  • Scientific research
  • Technology innovation
  • Design
  • Daily decision-making
  • Solving personal or professional problems

8. CREATIVE INTELLIGENCE - FIVE COMPETENCIES

Introduction to Knowledge Mining

Knowledge Mining explores different kinds of knowledge acquisition. It involves Embodiment (becoming aware of creative knowledge and skills) and Immersion (diving fully into topics until they become second nature). You don't need to be an expert to be a knowledge miner - just observe and explore. The more knowledge you mine, the more dots you connect and patterns you see.

1. EMBODIMENT

Embodiment begins with knowing yourself: who you are, your cultures, and what you want to create in the world. The ability to recognize unspoken aspirations of your groups and cultures is a skill many creative people share.

  • We often overlook knowledge we naturally possess from life experiences, not formal practice
  • This knowledge is intuitive, so we may not see it as "knowledge" we were taught
  • Embodiment is not required for creating, but it's a valuable pool of often underappreciated insight
  • Key point: Be aware of your skills, knowledge, beliefs, and gaps that may affect understanding new groups

2. IMMERSION

Not everyone wants to remain in their birth culture; we seek new experiences, people, and ways of life. Immersion allows learning about other groups' beliefs, habits, and skills.

  • Many creative individuals leave familiar worlds to gain new skills and perspectives
  • Immersion is the type of Knowledge Mining we are most familiar with
  • As students, we immerse in history, literature, science to understand them
  • Rarely do we see immersion as a creative competence
  • Knowledge gained through deep study can be mined to generate creativity
  • For creativity, it can be better to learn widely rather than focus only on becoming the best in one field
  • Society often discourages following curiosity; arts and "extraneous" subjects are underfunded
  • Immersion across diverse subjects builds essential knowledge dots for creative thinking
  • Immersion is open-ended and liberating: choose what interests you and gather as many knowledge dots as possible

3. CONNECTING DOTS

Connecting dots often starts by reviewing your own or others' past work and finding ways to create something new.

  • First step: Build bridges between seemingly unrelated islands of knowledge - also called lateral, relational, or horizontal thinking
  • Knowledge Mining doesn't require generating hundreds of random ideas like traditional brainstorming
  • Drawing from personal or organizational experiences to innovate sounds appealing but isn't always easy
  • With countless possibilities, deciding which dot to connect first can be challenging in a universe of infinite knowledge

4. DONUT KNOWLEDGE

Definition: Donut knowledge is the ability to see what isn't there.

  • How to gain it? Time - experience and exploration are key
  • This may involve leaving the familiar and going to new places, physically or intellectually, even if uncomfortable
  • Even if you've yet to amass experience in a particular field, you can still improve your chances of spotting surprises you may not be expecting

Strategies to Improve Donut Knowledge

Strategy Description
Know the Pattern, Watch for the Breaks Understand normal patterns to recognize anomalies
Bird the Birders Whatever your interests are, surround yourself with people who know how to look for the odd. This tends to increase your own chances of finding the rarity.

5. DREAMS, NOT NEEDS

Focuses on understanding what people find meaningful beyond their basic needs.

Key Ideas

  • Society often frames life in terms of needs, but humans are driven by dreams, longings, and meaning
  • Products like iPhones are not necessities, yet hold deep personal significance
  • Knowledge mining involves understanding what is meaningful and how it changes across cultures and generations
  • Encourages individuals and organizations to stay connected to the world and create meaningful offerings
Example: iPhones are not basic needs but carry deep personal significance and meaning for users.

Building Foundation for Creativity Through Knowledge Mining

Organizations and Knowledge Mining

  • Younger employees often bring ideas for new products, but experienced colleagues may overlook their value
  • Many organizations assume younger staff are there to learn and assist, not innovate
  • Overlooking employees' insights can be costly for organizations
  • Companies should audit employee skills and interests to identify creative potential
  • Policies like "percent time" or free time let employees work on projects they find meaningful
  • Investing in employees' creative time builds the organization's Creative Intelligence
  • Individuals should start by recognizing their own skills and experiences to improve knowledge mining
Example: Google's 20% time policy encouraged employees to spend one day a week on creative projects, producing successful services - until it was restricted to focus on Google+.

Creative Intelligence Components Summary

Competency Key Focus
Embodiment Knowing yourself
Immersion Learning deeply
Connecting Dots Linking ideas
Donut Knowledge Seeing what's missing
Dreams, Not Needs Creating meaning

Together, they form a powerful toolkit for innovation and creativity.

9. KEY EXAM CONCEPTS & QUESTIONS

Organizational Culture Questions

Q: What are the three requirements for culture to exist?

A: (1) Shared by vast majority of members, (2) Passed from generation to generation, (3) Shapes behaviour and perceptions

Q: What is the difference between observable and non-observable culture elements?

A: Observable: Practices, Language, Symbols. Non-observable: Norms, Values, Assumptions

Q: Describe the four types of organizational culture.

A: (1) Bureaucratic - formal rules, stability, (2) Clan - tradition, loyalty, socialization, (3) Market - measurable goals, performance-based, (4) Entrepreneurial - risk-taking, innovation

Diversity Questions

Q: What is the difference between surface-level and deep-level diversity?

A: Surface-level: Observable differences (age, gender, race). Deep-level: Non-observable differences (values, goals, personalities, skills)

Q: Name 5 benefits of workforce diversity.

A: (1) Increased productivity, (2) Increased creativity and problem solving, (3) Attract and retain talent, (4) Build synergy in teams, (5) Increases market share

Critical & Creative Thinking Questions

Q: What are the 6 steps of critical thinking?

A: (1) Identify problem, (2) Gather information, (3) Analyze facts, (4) Evaluate options, (5) Draw conclusion, (6) Reflect and learn

Q: How do critical and creative thinking work together?

A: Creative thinking generates ideas, while critical thinking filters and improves them. Together they create innovative solutions that are practical and effective.

Creative Intelligence Questions

Q: What are the 5 competencies of Creative Intelligence?

A: (1) Embodiment, (2) Immersion, (3) Connecting Dots, (4) Donut Knowledge, (5) Dreams, Not Needs

Q: What is Donut Knowledge?

A: The ability to see what isn't there. Gained through time, experience, and exploration.
